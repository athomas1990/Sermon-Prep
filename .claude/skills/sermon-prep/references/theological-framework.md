# Theological Framework

Reference for sermon structure, application, and doctrinal guardrails. Customize to match your tradition.

---

## Hermeneutical Approach

**Grammatical-historical interpretation.** The primary meaning of a passage is what the original author intended for the original audience. Application flows from meaning, not the other way around.

**Christ-centered, not Christ-forced.** Every passage exists within the larger redemptive story. Good preaching connects the text to the gospel. Bad preaching imports Jesus where the text does not go there.

**Descriptive vs. prescriptive.** Narratives describe what happened. They do not automatically prescribe what you should do. Flag this when the passage is narrative.

---

## Sermon Structure Principles

**One big idea.** Every sermon exists to say one thing. Sub-points support the big idea, they do not compete with it. If the sermon has two big ideas, it is two sermons.

**Text drives structure.** Let the shape of the passage inform the shape of the sermon. A list passage can use a list structure. A narrative passage often works as a story with a turn. Do not force every text into the same three-point mold.

**Application is specific.** "Trust God more" is not application. "Call the person you have been avoiding this week" is application. Push toward the concrete.

**Law before gospel, gospel before imperative.** Establish what God requires before announcing what God provides. Announce what God provides before asking what people should do. Moralizing skips the middle step.

---

## Doctrinal Guardrails

Stay in the evangelical mainstream on secondary issues unless a specific tradition is set in pastor-foundation.

**Hold firm:**
- Scripture as inspired and authoritative
- Salvation by grace through faith
- The bodily resurrection of Jesus
- The triune God

**Stay neutral (unless tradition specified):**
- Baptism mode and timing
- Spiritual gifts and their continuance
- Eschatological frameworks
- Gender roles in ministry

---

## Application Framework

Good application answers three questions:

1. **What does this mean?** (Interpretation)
2. **So what?** (Relevance — why this matters to these people now)
3. **Now what?** (Response — the specific thing you are asking people to do or believe differently)

Every sermon should move through all three. Most bad sermons stop at one or two.
