# Sermon Research: Mark 14:1-11

**Church:** First Presbyterian Church of Griffin
**Date:** April 26, 2026
**Series:** The Gospel of Mark

---

## Sermon Brief

**Big Idea:** What you do for Jesus will never outrun who you are with Jesus.

**Key Tension:** The disciples are not wrong that the perfume could have fed the poor. Their math is right. But Jesus does not correct their math. He reframes the category entirely. Useful ministry and intimate devotion are not the same thing, and when they compete, Jesus calls the intimate thing beautiful.

**Audience Need:** People who are active in their faith, maybe even busy in it, but who have quietly let the doing crowd out the being.

**Desired Response:** Identify one specific time in the day, morning, afternoon, or evening, and protect it as time with Jesus. Not a program, not a to-do. Just time. And ask the honest question: what changes in me because I do?

**The Turn:** The woman does something that looks strange and wasteful, and Jesus calls it the most important thing in the room. The unnamed woman, the one everyone in the passage is criticizing, is the only person in the story who actually understands who Jesus is.

**The Judas Contrast:** Mark brackets this story deliberately. Priests plotting to kill Jesus in verses 1-2. Judas going to betray him in verses 10-11. The anointing sits between two acts of betrayal. Same room. Opposite responses. The woman gives everything because she sees clearly. Judas, who has been with Jesus longer, sees nothing.

## 1. Passage Context

Mark is the shortest of the four Gospels, likely written around 65-70 AD for a Roman Gentile audience. The author, traditionally identified as John Mark, crafts a fast-paced narrative driven by action rather than extended discourse. The characteristic word of Mark is "immediately" (euthys), appearing over 40 times, and the gospel moves from scene to scene with urgency. Chapter 14 opens the Passion narrative, the account of Jesus's arrest, trial, and crucifixion, which occupies roughly a third of the entire book. The placement of this passage is deliberate: Jesus has just finished the Olivet Discourse in chapter 13, predicting the destruction of Jerusalem and his return. Now the narrative descends from prophecy into the events that will make it necessary.

The passage uses a literary device Mark employs throughout the gospel called **intercalation**, sometimes called a Markan sandwich. One story is placed inside another to force a comparison. Here, the anointing at Bethany (vv. 3-9) is bracketed by the priests' plot to kill Jesus (vv. 1-2) and Judas's decision to betray him (vv. 10-11). Mark is not arranging events accidentally. He wants the reader to hold all three scenes in view simultaneously: two acts of betrayal surrounding one act of extravagant devotion. The contrast is the point, and recognizing the structure is the first interpretive move a careful reader must make.

The Passover setting is theologically loaded. Passover commemorated Israel's liberation from Egypt, the foundational act of redemption in the Old Testament. Jerusalem's population swelled with pilgrims during the feast. It was the highest-profile week of the Jewish calendar, politically charged, and a time when Roman soldiers were on heightened alert. The chief priests' caution about arresting Jesus during the festival (v. 2) is pragmatic: they fear the crowd, not divine judgment. The irony Mark sets up is that the act of worship they are trying to prevent is happening quietly in a house in Bethany while they plot.

## 2. Historical and Cultural Background

Anointing carried deep cultural significance in the ancient Near East. The anointing of the head was the act performed over kings at their coronation: Samuel anointed David (1 Samuel 16:13), and Zadok anointed Solomon (1 Kings 1:39). When the unnamed woman pours nard over Jesus's head, she is performing a royal act whether she consciously understands it or not. Mark's first readers would have caught this connection immediately. Anointing the feet was an act of hospitality and honor. Anointing the head was an act of installation.

The perfume itself is worth understanding. Nard, or spikenard, was imported from the foothills of the Himalayas in northern India. Its rarity and transport costs made it extraordinarily expensive. Three hundred denarii was approximately a year's wages for a day laborer. The alabaster jar that contained it was sealed, meaning it could not be opened partially: breaking it was an irreversible, all-or-nothing act. The disciples' objection is not petty. They are watching the equivalent of a full year's salary poured out in a single gesture. They frame it as a charity question, which is a reasonable frame given the Torah's explicit commands to care for the poor. Their instinct is not wrong; their category is.

Jesus's response alludes directly to Deuteronomy 15:11: "There will always be poor people in the land. Therefore I command you to be openhanded toward your fellow Israelites who are poor and needy." Jesus is not dismissing the poor or contradicting the Torah. He is quoting the very verse that commands care for the poor, while making a different point: the opportunity to serve him in this particular way is closing. The poor will be present for future service. He will not be. This moment is kairos, a decisive and unrepeatable time, not chronos, ordinary sequential time.

The host, Simon the Leper, is identified by a name that marks his past rather than his present. Active leprosy would have made hosting a dinner party impossible under purity laws. Simon's label signals he was healed, most likely by Jesus, and that Jesus's table fellowship extended to those society had excluded. The house of a healed leper is exactly where a scene like this would unfold.

## 3. Key Word Studies

| English | Transliteration | Literal Meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| beautiful (thing) | kalon (kalos) | beautiful, noble, excellent, genuinely good | Carries aesthetic weight beyond just "morally acceptable." Jesus is not merely approving the act; he is calling it beautiful in a way that includes both moral and aesthetic dimensions. |
| pure nard | nardou pistikes | genuine/faithful spikenard, unadulterated | Pistikos (from pistos, faithful) marks this as the real thing, not a cheaper imitation. The genuineness of the substance mirrors the character of the devotion. |
| anointed (for burial) | murizein | to anoint with ointment or myrrh for burial preparation | Same verb in Mark 16:1, when the women bring spices to anoint Jesus after the crucifixion and find the tomb empty. She completed the burial anointing in time. They arrived too late. |
| waste | apoleia | destruction, ruin, perdition, waste | The same word used in John 17:12 for Judas as "the son of perdition." The word the disciples use for what the woman did is the same word Scripture uses to describe who Judas is. |
| gospel | euangelion | good news, gospel | Same word as Mark 1:1: "the beginning of the gospel of Jesus Christ." Her act is permanently woven into the gospel. Jesus's prediction is self-fulfilling. |

### Translation Comparison

**kalon (beautiful/good):**
- NIV: "a beautiful thing"
- ESV: "a beautiful thing"
- KJV: "a good work"
- NLT: "a good deed"
- NASB: "a good deed"

The NIV and ESV correctly capture the aesthetic dimension. KJV and NASB use "good" without the beauty layer.

## 4. Commentary Insights

**William Lane (NICNT, The Gospel of Mark)** emphasizes the Markan sandwich as intentional literary architecture, not coincidence of chronology. Lane sees the woman's act as carrying royal and messianic overtones: anointing the head is the gesture performed over kings, and Mark's reader is being invited to see this unnamed woman recognizing Jesus's kingship at the precise moment his enemies are conspiring against it. The disciples' blindness in this scene is consistent with the pattern of misunderstanding throughout Mark.

**James Edwards (Pillar NT Commentary, The Gospel According to Mark)** draws attention to the irony that the woman performs a prophetic act the disciples cannot. Three passion predictions have already occurred in Mark's gospel (8:31, 9:31, 10:33-34), and the disciples have failed to receive any of them. This woman, with no recorded theological instruction, enacts the burial anointing before the death. Edwards also connects Jesus's prediction in v. 9 to the opening verse of Mark: her act is woven into the beginning of the gospel and cannot be separated from it.

**R.T. France (NIGTC, The Gospel of Mark)** focuses on the Deuteronomy 15:11 allusion and the misreading it prevents. Jesus is not setting up a permanent hierarchy where personal devotion to him supersedes care for the poor. He is making a temporal claim: the window to serve him in this particular way is closing. France also notes that the adjective pistikos on the nard carries interpretive weight: the genuine quality of the perfume mirrors the genuine quality of the devotion, and both stand in contrast to what Judas is about to do.

**David Garland (NIV Application Commentary, Mark)** develops the Judas contrast at length. Judas has been in the inner circle for three years. He has witnessed miracles, heard extended teaching, and watched Jesus raise Lazarus from the dead. He walks out of the same dinner at Bethany and goes directly to betray Jesus for money. Garland reads this as Mark's climactic insider/outsider reversal: those closest to Jesus in physical proximity prove to be furthest from him in understanding and love. The unnamed woman sees most clearly.

**Warren Wiersbe (BE Series)** offers a pastoral observation: the thing the disciples call waste, Jesus calls beautiful. The categories we use to evaluate acts of devotion, useful versus useless, strategic versus impractical, are not always the categories Jesus uses. The alabaster jar had value. Breaking it was the point.

## 6. Theological Themes

### Costly Devotion
The alabaster jar is sealed and cannot be partially opened. Breaking it is an irreversible, all-or-nothing act. The woman gives everything at once with no possibility of retrieval. Jesus does not just accept the act; he calls it beautiful and says it will be remembered forever. For a congregation trained to evaluate everything by usefulness and return on investment, this passage directly challenges that framework.

### Knowing Jesus Rightly
The woman grasps something about Jesus's identity that those closest to him have missed. Three passion predictions have failed to land with the disciples. She enacts what they cannot receive. This is the goal of biblical literacy: the point of knowing the scriptures is knowing the person the scriptures reveal. Clarity about Jesus produces a certain kind of response. Clouded sight produces calculated self-interest.

### Proximity Is Not Intimacy
Judas has been closer to Jesus physically than almost anyone alive. He has heard every sermon, witnessed every miracle, and sat at every table. Proximity has not produced knowledge or love. For church members who have attended for years, this is a quiet pressure test. Showing up is not the same as being with him.

### The Unrepeatable Moment
Jesus tells the disciples the window to serve him in this way is closing: "you will not always have me." The woman responds to a kairos moment; the disciples argue about resource allocation. Most congregations have grown comfortable with the idea that the important things can always be done later. This passage creates urgency around the question of time with Jesus.

### Memory and the Gospel
Jesus embeds her act in the proclamation of the gospel itself: "wherever the gospel is proclaimed in the whole world, what she has done will be told in memory of her." He makes this promise while she is being criticized in the same room. She is not seeking recognition. He gives it anyway. What is done for Jesus out of genuine love, not for visibility or reputation, is permanently part of the story.

## 7. Thinking Prompts

1. Jesus says "she has done what she could" (v. 8). The disciples thought they were proposing something better she could have done with the money. What does it mean that Jesus defines her act as the fullest expression of what she was able to offer? How does your congregation define what it means to do "what they could"?

2. The disciples' objection is grounded in real concern for the poor, a genuine biblical value the Torah commands. How do you preach this without giving your congregation permission to deprioritize service and generosity in favor of personal devotion? The text does not pit the two against each other permanently.

3. Judas walked out of that dinner and went directly to betray Jesus. Something in the room, or something already in him, produced that decision. What does it tell you about what happens to a person who is near Jesus for years but has never actually arrived at who he is?

4. The woman is unnamed in Mark. Jesus says she will be remembered wherever the gospel is proclaimed. She is remembered, but not by name. How does your congregation define significance? What would it mean to do something that matters permanently but is never credited to you by name?

5. Jesus says "you will not always have me" as a correction to people who thought they were being thoughtful and generous. What has your congregation been deferring in their relationship with Jesus while managing concerns that also seem legitimate and important?

6. Mark leaves the woman unnamed. John 12 names her as Mary of Bethany. When parallel accounts have different details, how do you read them? This is a biblical literacy teaching moment: what do we gain from having both accounts, and what mistake do we make if we flatten them into one?

7. The word the disciples use for the perfume being "wasted" is apoleia, the same Greek word used in John 17:12 for Judas as "the son of perdition." Mark may be inviting the reader to ask: who in this scene is the one actually being destroyed?
