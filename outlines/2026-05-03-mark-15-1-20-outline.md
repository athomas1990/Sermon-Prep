# Sermon Outline: Mark 15:1-20

**Passage:** Mark 15:1-20
**Date:** May 3, 2026
**Church:** First Presbyterian Church of Griffin

**Big Idea:** Jesus said nothing before Pilate because there was nothing to argue. He had already decided to die in Barabbas' place.

---

## Introduction

**Hook:** Pilate has interrogated criminals his entire career. He knows how this goes. Men on trial for their lives beg, bargain, perform innocence, say anything to survive. Jesus says nothing. And Pilate, the most powerful man in the room, is stopped cold by it. Mark uses the word "amazed." This is not mild curiosity. This is a man who cannot make sense of what he is watching.

**Bridge:** Most of us read this trial as observers. We watch the crowd, shake our heads at Pilate, feel the injustice of the whole thing. But the text will not let us stay in the crowd. There is one other person in this story who is not watching. He is in a cell. And by the time this passage is over, he walks out free. His name is Barabbas, and he is the one this sermon is actually about.

---

## Movement 1: The Silence Is a Decision (vv. 1-5)

**Text:** "But Jesus still made no answer, so that Pilate was amazed." (v. 5)

**Observation:** Jesus answered the high priest directly. When Caiaphas asked if he was the Messiah, he said yes (14:62). He is not speechless. He is not afraid. Before Pilate, he offers one short answer to the first question, then nothing. The silence is chosen, complete, and deliberate.

**Key point:** Men fight for their lives. They argue, they explain, they make their case. Jesus does not because he is not trying to get out. He has already made the decision this trial is trying to force on him. There is nothing to argue because there is no verdict he is trying to change.

**Application:** Pilate is amazed because he has never seen a man who did not fight for his own life. The congregation has probably never thought of that silence as the loudest thing in the room. It is not resignation. It is resolution.

---

## Movement 2: The Exchange (vv. 6-15)

**Text:** "Do you want me to release to you the king of the Jews?" (v. 9) / "Then what shall I do with the one you call the king of the Jews?" (v. 12)

**Observation:** Barabbas is not presented as sympathetic. He committed insurrection and murder. He is guilty of the exact political crime Jesus is falsely accused of. The crowd, manipulated by the chief priests, asks for Barabbas. Pilate, knowing Jesus is innocent, hands him over anyway.

**The turn:** We watch this and feel the injustice. We position ourselves as the ones who would have chosen correctly. But we are not the crowd in this story. We are in the cell. Barabbas is every person in this room. Guilty, waiting, with no argument left. And someone outside is saying nothing so we can walk out.

**Key point:** The silence of verse 5 was not for Pilate's benefit. It was the sound of the decision already made. Jesus is not trying to save his own life because he has already decided to give it away. For a man named Barabbas. For you.

**Note on Barabbas:** His name means "son of the father." The actual Son of the Father goes to the cross so that a man who only bears the name can walk free. That is not a coincidence Mark would have missed.

**Application:** This is substitutionary atonement before it is a doctrine. It is a transaction. One man walks out of a cell. Another man walks into a death sentence in his place. The question for Sunday is not whether your congregation believes this. It is whether they have ever stood in the cell and felt it.

---

## Movement 3: The Mockery Tells the Truth (vv. 16-20)

**Text:** "They put a purple robe on him, then twisted together a crown of thorns and set it on him. And they began to call out to him, 'Hail, king of the Jews!'" (vv. 17-18)

**Observation:** The soldiers know what they are doing. The purple robe mimics the emperor's cloak. The crown mimics the laurel wreath. "Hail, King" is a parody of "Hail, Caesar." They are staging a mock coronation to humiliate him. Every element of it is accidentally correct.

**Key point:** The men trying to strip Jesus of dignity are, without knowing it, proclaiming who he is. The one who said nothing to save himself is in fact the King. The silence before Pilate was not weakness. The mocking of soldiers who think they have the last word is not the last word.

**Application:** The congregation walks out of this passage knowing something the soldiers did not. The crown was real. The king was real. And the man who said nothing so Barabbas could go free is the same one wearing that crown.

---

## Conclusion

**Restate the big idea:** Jesus said nothing before Pilate because there was nothing to argue. He had already decided to die in Barabbas' place.

**Call to response:** Barabbas did not earn his release. He did not argue for it. He did not understand it. He walked out of a cell he deserved to be in because someone else chose to stay. The gratitude you are looking for, the weight of it, does not come from understanding the doctrine. It comes from standing in the cell. This week, pray from that place. Not "thank you for dying for sins in general." Thank you for saying nothing when you could have said everything. For me.

**Closing image:** Barabbas walks out into the sunlight. He did not negotiate that. He did not earn it. He just walked out free. That is the posture.
